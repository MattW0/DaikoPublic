800 years ago, on a continent called [[Maps/Daiko|Daiko]], the clever and craving humankind, discovered something dark and radiant deep beneath the earth's crust: **[[History/Lore/Obsidian|Obsidian]]**. Drawn by its arcane potential, they learned to process it, unlocking unprecedented access to the [[History/Lore/Weave|Weave]]. The strong became mages. The weak became their soldiers. And tribes formed into small kingdoms. Thus began the **[[History/Lore/Ages/Age of Dominion|Age of Dominion]]**.

The humans claimed the godless magic for themselves and spread like wind-blown fire across the land, exploring lands far west and north. In their hunger for control, they traded and clashed with foreign powers, unsettling the balance of ancient magical races. Those beings—fey, elemental, celestial—watched the rise of Obsidian with wary eyes, and either bent the knee or fled deeper into exile.

However, the human kingdoms, wielded, consumed, and hoarded [[History/Lore/Obsidian|Obsidian]] in excess. It was their fuel for conquest and sorcery—but it drank from the soul and consumed them. Many of them fell to its dark temptations or waged wars amongst themselves. Only the strongest kingdoms, with abundant sources of the arcane material prevailed. From that dark history and continuous war, colonization and exploitation, humans had to accept that "Those who use [[History/Lore/Obsidian|Obsidian]] for too long are changed—first in thought, then in flesh. And always, in allegiance."

Entering the [[History/Lore/Ages/Age of Enlightenment|Age of Enlightenment]] 300 years ago, the [[Nations/Daicon Kingdom/Daicon Kingdom|Daicon Kingdom]] was the first to discover, that [[History/Lore/Obsidian|Obsidian]]'s arcane energy could animate material itself. The result was a surge of innovation—engines, weapons, or more recently, **[[Nations/Daicon Kingdom/Lore/Magitech|Magitech]] automatons**, seeped into the daily lives and took over nearly every laboring task. With less need for manual forced labor and foreign products, colonizing efforts became less attractive. 
With their focus for development turned inward, society prospered and other kingdoms feared the power the Daicons achieved. They scrambled to imitate their designs, yet without Daiconian refinement methods—and without control over their inner corruption—their attempts often ended in ruin. One by one, the human nations collapsed and only three endured. Amongst them the [[Nations/Daicon Kingdom/Daicon Kingdom|Daicon Kingdom]], disciplined, ordered, and rich from their dominance in arcane industry.

It was in the closing decades of the [[History/Lore/Ages/Age of Enlightenment|Age of Enlightenment]] that the Daicons began to tighten their grip on the one thing they still feared: **magic itself**. Any kind of extraction, trade and use of [[History/Lore/Obsidian|Obsidian]] became highly controlled and regulated. The largest industrial conglomerate established its economic power as a monopoly with close ties to the monarchy and its council. It was them, publicly known as [[Nations/The Gray Ring/The Grey Ring|The Grey Ring]], that suggested and formed the [[Nations/The Gray Ring/Lore/MICE|MICE]] agency 20 years ago. 

MICE as in "Magic & Intelligence Control Enforcement" grew from a regulatory body to a militant agency as we enter the [[History/Lore/Ages/Age of Shadows|Age of Shadows]]. With their brutal authoritarian power the agents can move and act freely in the [[Nations/Daicon Kingdom/Daicon Kingdom|Daicon Kingdom]] today. Even the Queen—**[[Nations/Daicon Kingdom/NPCs/Elen the Radiant|Elen the Radiant]]**, long beloved and now more symbol than sovereign—has little sway over [[Nations/The Gray Ring/Lore/MICE|MICE]]’s true operations, much less over what [[Nations/The Gray Ring/The Grey Ring|The Grey Ring]] decides to be the next best thing for their shareholders. 

In towns like **[[Nations/Daicon Kingdom/Towns/Dunmarrow/Dunmarrow|Dunmarrow]]**, on the edge of Daiconian reach, the shadows are thinner, but even here, [[Nations/The Gray Ring/The Grey Ring|The Grey Ring]] casts a long silhouette.
